Laser toning, IPL, and pico lasers get talked about like a menu of interchangeable "pigment lasers," but they're built on different devices with different pulse behavior. What ties them together isn't the machine — it's a single optical idea: pick a color of light that one target in the skin absorbs far more readily than everything around it, so that target heats up (or gets mechanically disrupted) while nearby tissue is relatively spared. Once that idea clicks, the differences between the three start to make a lot more sense.

## Quick answer

Laser toning, IPL, and pico devices are all built around selective photothermolysis — choosing a wavelength or light spectrum that pigment (melanin) or blood vessels absorb more than the surrounding skin does, so the target is affected while the tissue around it is left relatively undisturbed. Laser toning uses a single low-energy wavelength over many gentle sessions; IPL uses broadband light — not a single-wavelength laser — that targets both brown and red discoloration at once; pico lasers use extremely short pulses that act more mechanically to break pigment apart. All three carry a real risk of temporary darkening of the treated skin, particularly for deeper or tanned skin tones and with unprotected sun exposure. Response varies from person to person, several sessions are typical, and results are not permanent. A licensed professional is the right person to assess suitability for any individual's skin.

## Why people get it

The concerns that lead people toward pigment treatments are usually visual and specific: melasma patches that seem to deepen with sun or hormonal shifts, old acne marks that took on a brown or reddish cast, sun spots that showed up gradually over years, or an overall unevenness in skin tone that makeup only partly covers. These are extremely common concerns, not signs that something has gone wrong with someone's skin — pigment production is uneven by nature and reacts to sun exposure, inflammation, hormones, and genetics in ways that differ from person to person.

What draws people to light-based treatments specifically is the promise of addressing pigment at the level where it actually sits — in the epidermis or dermis — rather than only fading it from the surface with topical products. That's a meaningfully different approach, and it's also one with real risks attached, which is exactly why it's worth understanding the underlying mechanism, and the honest caveats, before treating any of these names as interchangeable.

## How it works

The principle behind all three treatments is selective photothermolysis, which sounds technical but describes something fairly intuitive: different substances in the skin absorb different colors of light to different degrees. Melanin, the pigment responsible for brown and tan coloring, and hemoglobin, the pigment in blood that gives redness its color, each absorb certain wavelengths much more strongly than the surrounding skin does. By choosing light tuned to what the target absorbs, a device can heat — or otherwise disrupt — that target while leaving nearby tissue comparatively undisturbed.

**Laser toning** typically refers to a low-energy Q-switched Nd:YAG laser operating at 1064 nanometers, delivered over many sessions at settings gentle enough to avoid an open wound. It's discussed most often in the context of melasma and general tone-evening, where a large number of low-intensity passes is understood to gradually affect pigment-producing cells with less risk of triggering the skin's own darkening response than a single higher-intensity pass would. The gentleness is deliberate, but toning is not risk-free in the other direction either: repeated or overly aggressive low-fluence sessions carry a recognized risk of the opposite problem — patchy loss of pigment (hypopigmentation, sometimes appearing as small lighter spots) or a rebound of the very pigment being treated. It's one reason toning is generally approached as a slow, monitored course rather than something to rush.

**IPL, or intense pulsed light,** works differently from a laser at a fundamental level. Where a laser emits a single, precise wavelength, IPL emits a broad spectrum of light — a range of wavelengths at once, filtered to emphasize certain bands — which is why it's accurate to call IPL a light-based treatment but not accurate to call it "a laser." That broadband quality lets a single IPL session address both brown pigment and red, vessel-related discoloration in the same pass, since different wavelengths within that broad spectrum are absorbed by each.

**Pico lasers** take a different approach to delivering energy: rather than relying mainly on heat, they deliver energy in picosecond-scale pulses — pulses so short they are measured in trillionths of a second. That speed is understood to work more mechanically than thermally, shattering pigment particles into much smaller fragments that the body can clear over time, with less heat transferred to surrounding tissue than older, longer-pulse technologies.

Across all three, the shared reality is that pigment cells and the skin's inflammatory response are closely linked. Any treatment that disturbs pigment can, in provoking a healing response, temporarily produce more pigment before things settle — which is the mechanism behind the darkening risk described below.

## How the options differ

| Treatment | Light type | Main target | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Laser toning | Low-energy Q-switched Nd:YAG, 1064 nm (single wavelength) | Pigment and overall tone | Many gentle sessions rather than one intense pass |
| IPL | Broadband light (a filtered spectrum, not a single wavelength — not technically a laser) | Pigment and vessels together | Can address brown and red discoloration in the same session |
| Pico | Picosecond-pulse laser | Stubborn or deeper pigment | Acts more mechanically than thermally, breaking pigment into finer fragments |

This table describes the underlying principle behind each option, not a ranking of which works better. Which mechanism is relevant to a particular concern, skin tone, and pigment type is a question for a licensed professional working from a direct assessment, not something a comparison table can settle.

## What to know going in

This is the section worth reading slowly, because pigment treatments carry a real and specific risk that's worth naming plainly: **post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation**, or PIH — a temporary darkening of the treated skin that can appear in the days or weeks after a session, as part of the skin's own healing and inflammatory response. PIH is not a rare complication; it is a well-recognized possibility with any treatment that disturbs pigment or provokes inflammation in the skin, and it can take weeks to months to fade.

A few honest points follow from that:

- **Risk is not evenly distributed.** PIH is understood to occur more often, and can be more pronounced, in deeper or more tanned skin tones, because that skin tends to produce a stronger pigment response to inflammation than lighter skin does. This is a real consideration, not a minor footnote, for anyone with medium-to-deep or tanned skin weighing these treatments.
- **Sun exposure raises the risk substantially.** Ultraviolet exposure before or after a session is one of the most significant factors in triggering or worsening PIH, which is why diligent, daily sun protection before and after treatment is not optional advice — it's central to how these treatments are used safely.
- **Results are not immediate or assured.** Multiple sessions, spaced weeks apart, are typical for any of these approaches, and how much visible change any individual sees varies widely depending on the type and depth of pigment, skin tone, and how the skin responds.
- **Results are not permanent.** New pigment can form over time from ongoing sun exposure, hormonal factors, or the natural aging process, which is why maintenance sessions are common for people who want to sustain an even tone.
- **Suitability is an individual medical question.** A licensed professional can assess skin tone, pigment type, and history of PIH or other reactions in a way a general guide cannot, and that assessment should come before any decision.

## After-care at home

Because pigment-focused treatments carry a meaningful risk of triggering more pigment if the skin is irritated or unprotected afterward, after-care at home leans even more heavily on calm, protective habits than it does for other treatment types.

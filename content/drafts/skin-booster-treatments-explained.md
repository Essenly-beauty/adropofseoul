Rejuran, skin botox, and Juvelook get lumped together as "skin boosters," but they're not the same material doing the same job. What they share is the delivery method, not the ingredient: very small, closely spaced injections that place a substance into the dermis — the living layer under the surface — rather than onto the skin like a serum. What's actually in the needle, and what it's meant to do once it's there, differs from one to the next, and that's worth untangling before any of the names start to blur together.

## Quick answer

Skin boosters are a family of micro-injection treatments that deliver a material into the dermis to support texture, hydration, or firmness, usually as a course of several sessions rather than a single visit. Rejuran uses polynucleotides to support skin repair; skin botox uses a tiny, spread-out dose of botulinum toxin aimed at surface qualities like oil and pore appearance, not muscle movement; Juvelook and similar biostimulators use a material that prompts the skin to gradually build its own collagen. Each is understood to work by a different mechanism, results build up over a course of treatments and fade over time, and individual response varies. A licensed professional is the right person to advise on suitability.

## Why people get it

People usually come to skin boosters from a slightly different place than they come to lifting treatments. The concern is less "my skin has started to sag" and more "my skin looks tired, dull, dehydrated, or rough in a way moisturizer doesn't fully fix." Texture, tone evenness, and a kind of overall thinness or dullness in the skin are the usual triggers, and they tend to show up gradually rather than as a single moment of noticing.

The appeal of an injectable approach, as opposed to topical skincare, is depth: a cream can only do so much once it's sitting on the surface, while an injection can place material directly into the layer where some of these qualities — hydration, elasticity, density — actually originate. That's a meaningfully different delivery method, and it comes with meaningfully different considerations, which is exactly why it's worth understanding what's being injected rather than assuming "skin booster" describes one single thing.

## How it works

The common thread across Rejuran, skin botox, and Juvelook is the injection method itself: a series of very small, shallow injections spread across the treatment area, placing tiny amounts of material into the dermis rather than delivering one larger volume in one spot. That's a different approach from a single filler injection, and it's part of why these treatments are typically done as a course — several sessions spaced some weeks apart — rather than as a one-time procedure.

What's inside the needle is where the three diverge:

**Rejuran** relies on polynucleotides — fragments of DNA material derived from salmon that has been processed for cosmetic and medical use. These fragments are understood to work by supporting the skin's own repair processes, encouraging the tissue environment associated with hydration and elasticity. The idea is less "adding volume" and more "supporting the skin's underlying repair machinery."

**"Skin botox," sometimes called microtox,** uses botulinum toxin — the same broad category of ingredient used in wrinkle-relaxing injections — but in a genuinely different way. Traditional botulinum toxin injections for expression lines are placed into specific muscles, in doses calculated to reduce that muscle's movement. Skin botox instead uses much smaller amounts spread intradermally — into the skin itself, across a broad area — with the aim of affecting surface qualities like oil production, pore appearance, and overall skin smoothness, rather than relaxing muscle movement. The technique, the dosing, the depth, and the goal are all different from a standard wrinkle treatment, even though the underlying ingredient category shares a name. Confusing the two is an easy mistake, but they're not interchangeable procedures.

**Juvelook**, along with similar biostimulator products, is built around a poly-lactic-acid-type material, sometimes combined with hyaluronic acid. Rather than acting immediately, this material is understood to work as a gradual stimulus: it prompts the body to build up its own collagen over time in the treated area, which is why any firming effect from a biostimulator tends to appear slowly, over weeks to months, rather than right after the session.

All three approaches rely on the body's own biological response — repair, surface change, or collagen synthesis — rather than instantly reshaping the skin, which is part of why none of them are one-and-done procedures.

## How the options differ

| Treatment | What's injected | What it aims to improve | Sessions |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rejuran | Polynucleotides (salmon-DNA-derived fragments) | Skin repair, elasticity, hydration | Typically a course of several sessions |
| Skin botox (microtox) | Micro-dose botulinum toxin, placed intradermally across a broad area | Oil production, pore appearance, surface smoothness | Typically repeated at intervals |
| Juvelook (and similar biostimulators) | Poly-lactic-acid-type material, sometimes with hyaluronic acid | Gradual firmness via the skin's own collagen production | Typically a course of several sessions |

This table describes the underlying principle behind each option, not a ranking. Which mechanism is relevant to a given person's skin and goals is a question for a licensed professional, not something a comparison table can settle on its own.

## What to know going in

Because these are injections, some short-term reaction at the injection sites is common and expected: tiny needle marks, pinpoint redness, or mild swelling that typically settles within a short period, though exactly how long varies from person to person.

A few things apply across all three options:

- Results build gradually and cumulatively. Most of these treatments are designed as a course of sessions, not a single visit, and the improvement people notice tends to accumulate across that course rather than appearing all at once.
- Results are not permanent. The materials break down or the effect fades over time, which is why repeat sessions are typical for anyone who wants to maintain the look rather than a one-time change.
- Individual response varies. Skin condition, age, the specific product used, and general health all affect how much difference any person notices, and no outcome can be predicted in advance.
- Suitability, including whether skin botox is appropriate given someone's history with muscle-relaxing injections elsewhere on the face, is an individual medical question for a licensed professional to assess, not something a general guide can settle.

## After-care at home

In the days after any of these treatments, the priority at home is usually the same regardless of which option was used: keep the skin calm, avoid unnecessary irritation, and let the injection sites settle on their own timeline.

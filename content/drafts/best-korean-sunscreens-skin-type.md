## What actually matters before you buy

Skip the marketing and check three things on the label.

**SPF and PA rating.** SPF blocks UVB (the burn), PA+ ratings block UVA (the aging, the reason your skin looks tired at 35). Korean sunscreens are regulated for real SPF testing, unlike some US drugstore labels — look for SPF 50+ PA++++ for daily city wear.

**Chemical vs. mineral filters.** Most Korean sunscreens use chemical filters (Uvinul, Tinosorb) because they're thinner and blend clear. Mineral (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) sits on top of skin and is gentler for reactive skin, but is more likely to leave a cast. Hybrid formulas try to split the difference.

**No-white-cast claims are not all equal.** "No white cast" on packaging usually means "no white cast on pale-to-medium Korean skin tones under a store's studio lighting." On deeper skin tones or in direct sun, some of these still gray up. I tested each one in direct daylight, not just indoors.

> **How we tested**
> Five sunscreens, each worn for at least two full weeks in place of a daily SPF, checked at three points: application (white cast, pilling under makeup), midday (oil breakthrough, re-apply feel), and photos in direct sun. [[ NOTE]]

## FAQ

**What's the best Korean sunscreen for oily skin?**
Beauty of Joseon Relief Sun is the most recommended for a reason — it dries down closest to matte and layers cleanly under makeup, though it's not a full oil-control sunscreen if you're heavily oily by midday.

**What's the best Korean sunscreen for sensitive skin?**
Isntree's Hyaluronic Acid Watery Sun Gel and Skin1004's Centella Sun Cream are the two lowest-fuss options here — simple formulas without heavy fragrance or added actives that can trigger a reaction.

**Do Korean sunscreens really have no white cast?**
Most of the hybrid and chemical-filter formulas above blend clear on light-to-medium skin tones in normal indoor light. In direct sun or on deeper skin tones, check swatches first — "no white cast" is a spectrum, not a guarantee.

**How much sunscreen should I actually use?**
About a quarter-teaspoon (roughly a nickel-sized amount) for the face alone — most people apply less than half that, which is the real reason SPF underperforms in daily life, not the formula.

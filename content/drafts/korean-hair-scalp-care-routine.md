My hairdresser in Seoul spent the first ten minutes of a ₩[[ NOTE]] cut looking at my part line under a scope, the way a dermatologist looks at pores. She wasn't checking for split ends — she was checking my scalp, for oil buildup, flaking, a couple of clogged follicles she pointed at on the screen without much sympathy.

That's the whole premise of Korean hair care: the scalp is skin, and it gets treated like skin. Cleansed, exfoliated, treated, monitored — not just washed and conditioned and forgotten. Once you see it that way, the "extra step" routine you see online actually makes sense.

## The core idea: scalp first, strands second

Western hair care mostly asks "is my hair damaged?" Korean hair care asks "is my scalp healthy?" first, on the logic that hair grown from a clogged, inflamed, or overly oily scalp was never going to look good no matter what you put on the ends.

In practice that means:

- **Scalp scaling** (a deeper exfoliation, roughly monthly) to remove sebum and product buildup a regular shampoo won't touch.
- **Scalp tonics and ampoules** applied to the roots after washing, not the lengths — similar in spirit to a face serum.
- **Wash frequency** that's often higher than the "wash less" advice common elsewhere, because a clean scalp is the priority over avoiding water contact with hair.
- **Cool or lukewarm rinses**, since hot water is treated as scalp-irritating the same way it's treated as skin-irritating.

## How often do Koreans actually wash their hair?

Daily, mostly — which surprises people who've absorbed the "don't wash your hair every day" rule from Western forums. Korean dermatology guidance generally treats the scalp as an extension of facial skin that needs regular cleansing, especially given Seoul's humidity and pollution. Skipping washes to "preserve oils" isn't really part of the local logic. [[ NOTE]] That doesn't mean stripping shampoo daily — it means a gentler, scalp-focused cleanser used often, paired with a heavier treatment less frequently.

## The scalp scaling step, explained

This is the one with no clean Western equivalent. Scalp scaling is a pre-shampoo exfoliation — a gel or peeling treatment massaged into the roots to loosen dead skin, oil, and styling product before the actual wash. Think of it as a chemical-exfoliant night, but for your roots.

**Aromatica's rosemary scalp scrub** is one of the most recommended over-the-counter versions for doing this at home — a coarse, herbal-smelling scrub meant to be worked into a dry or damp scalp before shampooing. [[ NOTE]]

## The products that actually show up in Korean bathrooms

Three names come up constantly, none of them boutique or hard to find:

- **Ryo** — the herbal, medicinal-smelling shampoo that's been a drugstore staple for decades, often the first "real" shampoo teenagers get handed for an oily scalp.
- **Dr.FORHAIR** — leans clinical, positioned around folliculitis and thinning, sold through pharmacies as much as beauty stores.
- **La'dor** — the glossy, damage-repair side, popular for keratin and protein treatments on color-treated or heat-styled hair.

## Myth vs. reality

**Myth:** Korean hair care is mostly about "glass hair" shine from fancy oils.
**Reality:** The shine is downstream of scalp health. Most of the actual routine — tonics, scaling, wash frequency — happens at the root and never touches the ends.

## The honest part: what didn't work

Scalp scaling products can be genuinely uncomfortable if your scalp is already sensitized — the exfoliating particles or menthol-heavy tonics that feel great on an oily scalp can sting on a dry, flaky one. [[ NOTE]]

Also worth saying plainly: none of this replaces treatment for actual hair loss or a diagnosed scalp condition. If you're dealing with persistent shedding, see a dermatologist — a rosemary scrub won't out-perform minoxidil or a real diagnosis.

## FAQ

**How often do Koreans wash their hair?**
Most people wash daily with a gentle, scalp-targeted shampoo, using lukewarm rather than hot water, then layer heavier treatments in less frequently.

**What is scalp scaling and do I need it?**
It's a pre-shampoo exfoliation for the scalp, usually done about monthly, aimed at removing buildup a regular wash misses. It's most useful if your scalp feels oily, flaky, or itchy despite washing regularly.
